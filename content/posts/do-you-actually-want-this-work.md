## Hunger Matters {#hunger-matters}

Intelligence matters. Credentials matter. But desire matters more.

A degree says, "I am capable." It is a backward-looking credential verifying what you have already done.

A portfolio says, "I have already started." It is a present-tense proof of concept showing what you can do right now.

But desire says, "I will keep doing this work when it becomes tedious, confusing, or unglamorous."

Desire defines who we are at the very deepest level.


## The Escape Hatch {#the-escape-hatch}

An example.

People talk about PhDs trying to transition into software engineering. It's not easy, and it's deeply frustrating. I get it. Academia routinely overproduces PhDs, and seeing that talent underutilized can feel like a tragic waste.

But a company is not obligated to convert academic talent into business value. A business is an engine for generating market utility, not an asylum for misallocated credentials.

If a business needs a software engineer who can work in a JavaScript framework, then that is the job.

The business problem is not "find the smartest person in the room." The business problem is "find someone who wants to build, maintain, debug, ship, and support the thing we need."

A business may very well look at a PhD and think, "This guy really wants (or wanted) something else. What does he want now?"

Every profession is 20% innovation and 80% maintenance.

Credentials chase the 20%.

Only desire survives the 80%.


## It Cuts Both Ways {#it-cuts-both-ways}

When transitioning academics get stuck, the common defense is: "A PhD could easily learn this entire tech stack in three months."

Really?

But that logic cuts both ways: if a stack is easy enough to learn on the company's dime, then it's easy enough to learn on your own time.

The refusal to build before you're paid isn't a lack of time; it is a lack of appetite.

If you want the role, prove it before you apply:

**Build something.**

**Push code.**

**Write tests.**

**Make a small app.**

**Document the process.**

Can you show that software is not merely a backup plan? Can you prove that this job isn't just a financial escape hatch after academia didn't work out?

Companies hate being someone's consolation prize.


## The Axe and the Trees {#the-axe-and-the-trees}

The critical question hiding underneath every hiring decision is simple: Do you actually want this work?

Not the title. Not the salary. The work.

Jordan Peterson observed, "there are some men, if you put an axe in their hands, all they want to do is chop down trees."

The same can be said for those who build software. They enjoy the language, the framework, the tooling, the debugging, and the strange little puzzles of the daily grind. They don't view the tools as a means to an end; they view the codebase as the destination. They are like the men who, if you put an axe in their hands, want nothing else but to chop down trees.

They may not have the fanciest academic credentials, but they have the hunger.

Given two candidates—one with immense academic brilliance but uncertain commitment, and another who has spent their weekends building software simply because they couldn't stop themselves---it is entirely rational for a company to choose the second candidate.

That isn't harsh. It is just real.

Capability is good.

Initiative is better.

But desire is what turns raw potential into actual value.
